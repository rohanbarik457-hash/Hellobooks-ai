# Profit and Loss Statement

**Description**: A primary financial statement that summarizes the revenues, costs, and expenses incurred during a specific period.

1. **Revenue**: The total amount of income generated by the sale of goods or services before any deductions. Example: A bookstore selling $5,000 worth of books in a single month.
2. **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: The direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold in a company. Example: A baker spending $200 on flour and sugar to make the bread they sold for $800.
3. **Gross Profit**: The profit a company makes after deducting the costs associated with making and selling its products. Example: If revenue is $1,000 and COGS is $400, the gross profit is $600.
4. **Operating Expenses**: The costs required to run the day-to-day operations of the business. Example: Paying $1,500 every month for office rent and utilities.
5. **Net Profit**: The actual profit after all expenses, including taxes and interest, have been paid. Example: After paying for the product, staff, rent, and taxes, a store has $1,200 left as actual profit.
6. **Administrative Expenses**: General overhead costs not directly tied to manufacturing or sales. Example: The $3,000 monthly salary paid to the company's office manager.
7. **Marketing Expenses**: Costs associated with promoting and selling products or services. Example: Spending $500 on Facebook ads to attract more customers to an online store.
8. **Financial Expenses**: Costs incurred from borrowing funds, typically interest payments. Example: Paying $150 in interest every month on a $10,000 business loan.
9. **Interest Income**: Revenue earned from interest on cash held in bank accounts or loans made to others. Example: Receiving a $10 monthly interest payment from the company savings account.
10. **Tax Expenses**: The financial liability owed to the government based on the company's earnings. Example: Setting aside 20% of monthly profit to pay the annual corporate tax bill.
11. **Earnings Before Tax (EBT)**: Financial performance calculated by subtracting all expenses except taxes from revenue. Example: A business having $8,000 in profit before the government takes its share.
12. **Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT)**: A measure of profit that excludes interest and income tax expenses. Example: Determining the core profitability of a factory before looking at its bank loans.
13. **Profit Margin**: A profitability ratio showing net profit as a percentage of total revenue. Example: Making $10 profit on a $100 sale results in a 10% profit margin.
14. **Variable Costs**: Expenses that change in direct proportion to how much a company produces or sells. Example: Spending more on cardboard boxes in months when more orders are shipped.
15. **Fixed Costs**: Expenses that remain the same regardless of the volume of production or sales. Example: Paying the same $2000 rent every month even if the store makes zero sales.
16. **Break-even Point**: The level of sales where total revenue exactly equals total expenses. Example: A coffee shop needing to sell 500 lattes to cover its monthly rent and staff costs.
17. **Operating Income**: Profit realized from a business's own operations after deducting operating expenses. Example: A software company's profit from its app subscriptions before including bank interest.
18. **Non-Operating Income**: Profits gained from activities not related to the core business operations. Example: A grocery store making $1,000 by selling an old delivery truck.
19. **Depreciation Expense**: Allocating the cost of a long-term asset over its useful life. Example: Recording a $1,000 "wear and tear" cost for a factory machine every year.
20. **Amortization Expense**: Gradually writing off the cost of an intangible asset like a patent or software. Example: Splitting the $5,000 cost of a 5-year software license into $1,000 yearly expenses.
21. **Contribution Margin**: The amount of revenue remaining after subtracting variable costs. Example: Selling a burger for $10 where ingredients cost $4 leaves a $6 contribution margin.
22. **Other Income**: Miscellaneous revenue that doesn't fit into primary categories. Example: A local cafe receiving a $500 award for "Best Small Business" in the city.
23. **Bad Debt Expense**: The cost incurred when a customer fails to pay their bill. Example: Writing off a $300 invoice because the client went bankrupt and cannot pay.
24. **Rent Expense**: The cost of using property or space owned by someone else. Example: Paying $800 a month to use a small studio for a photography business.
25. **Utility Expense**: Costs for electricity, water, gas, and waste disposal. Example: A $150 monthly bill to keep the lights and heat on in the office.
26. **Wages and Salaries**: The total amount paid to employees for their work. Example: Paying $4,000 in combine monthly wages to two part-time store assistants.
27. **Insurance Expense**: Monthly or annual payments made to protect against financial loss. Example: Paying $100 a month for "Liability Insurance" to protect the business from lawsuits.
28. **Repair and Maintenance**: Costs to keep business assets in good working condition. Example: Spending $200 to fix a broken office printer.
29. **Travel Expense**: Business-related costs for transportation, lodging, and meals. Example: Expensing a $50 train ticket used to visit a new client's office.
30. **Office Supplies**: Smaller items needed for daily work like paper, pens, and toner. Example: Buying $50 worth of envelopes and stamps for the mailing department.
31. **Professional Fees**: Payments made to outside experts like lawyers or accountants. Example: Paying an accountant $300 to file the company's yearly tax return.
32. **Gross Margin Percentage**: Gross profit divided by total revenue, expressed as a percent. Example: A baker with a 60% gross margin because ingredients only cost 40% of the sale price.
33. **Return on Sales (ROS)**: A measure of how much profit is produced per dollar of sales. Example: A company with an ROS of 15% makes 15 cents in profit for every $1 sold.
34. **Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A)**: Combined total of all non-production costs. Example: Adding up the rent, manager salaries, and advertising into one big operational category.
35. **Net Loss**: Occurs when total expenses are higher than total revenue during a period. Example: Spending $6,000 to run a business that only brought in $4,000 in sales.
36. **Bottom Line**: A common nickname for Net Profit because it appears at the very end of the statement. Example: A manager saying the "bottom line" is great because the business is making more profit than last year.
37. **Top Line**: A common nickname for Revenue because it appears at the very beginning of the statement. Example: A company focusing on "top line growth" by trying to get more customers and sales.
38. **Income Statement**: Another common name for the Profit and Loss (P&L) statement. Example: A bank asking for a copy of the "Income Statement" to confirm the business is profitable.
39. **P&L Reporting Period**: The specific timeframe covered by the statement (e.g., month, quarter, or year). Example: Looking at the "Q3 P&L" to see how the business performed during the summer months.
40. **Indirect Production Costs**: Factory expenses not directly tied to a specific unit of product. Example: The cost of heating the factory building where products are made.
41. **Inventory Shrinkage**: Loss of products due to theft, damage, or counting errors. Example: Realizing that 5 shirts are missing from the store shelf at the end of the month.
42. **Sales Discounts Expense**: Reductions in revenue due to offering lower prices to customers. Example: Losing $100 in potential revenue because you gave a 10% discount to regular clients.
43. **Sales Returns and Allowances**: Adjustments to revenue for items that customers brought back. Example: Refunding $50 to a customer who returned a defective coffee maker.
44. **Employee Benefits Expense**: Additional costs for workers beyond their base salary. Example: Paying $200 a month toward an employee's health insurance plan.
45. **Payroll Taxes**: The employer's share of taxes paid to the government for having workers. Example: Paying an extra 7.6% on top of an employee's salary for Social Security taxes.
46. **Subscription Expense**: Recurring costs for software or services used by the business. Example: Paying $20 monthly for a cloud storage service like Dropbox or Google Drive.
47. **Bank Fees**: Charges from financial institutions for maintaining an account or moving money. Example: Recording a $15 monthly "Management Fee" charged by the business bank.
48. **Legal Fees**: Costs for hiring attorneys to review contracts or give legal advice. Example: Spending $1,000 to have a lawyer write a new employment agreement.
49. **Postage and Shipping**: Costs to send documents or products through the mail. Example: Spending $20 to courier a signed contract to a partner in another state.
50. **Training and Development**: Money spent to improve the skills of employees. Example: Paying $500 for a staff member to attend a weekend workshop on digital marketing.
51. **Gain on Sale of Assets**: Profit made when an asset is sold for more than its book value. Example: Selling an office desk for $200 when the books showed its value as only $150.
52. **Loss on Sale of Assets**: Occurs when an asset is sold for less than its book value. Example: Selling a laptop for $300 when the accounting records said it was worth $500.
53. **Interest Coverage Ratio**: A measure of a company's ability to pay its debt interest with its profit. Example: A ratio of 10 means the company makes 10 times more profit than it owes in interest.
54. **EBITDA**: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Example: Using EBITDA to see how much cash the core business is truly generating before accounting rules.
55. **Pre-tax Income**: Total profit after operational expenses but before the government's share is taken. Example: Having $10,000 left at the end of the year, but $2,000 of that must go toward taxes.
56. **Extraordinary Items**: Unusual gains or losses that are highly unlikely to happen again. Example: A business losing $10,000 due to a rare earthquake that destroyed its inventory.
57. **Cost of Services**: The direct cost of providing a service, equivalent to COGS for products. Example: A consulting firm paying a freelancer $50/hour for work they bill at $150/hour.
58. **Licensing Revenue**: Money earned from letting others use your brand or intellectual property. Example: A famous illustrator getting $1,000 for allowing their drawings to be put on t-shirts.
59. **Research and Development (R&D)**: Money spent on creating new products or improving old ones. Example: A tech startup spending $20,000 on software engineers to build a new feature.
60. **Entertainment Expense**: Costs for hosting clients or partners for business purposes. Example: Taking a potential VIP client out for a $100 lunch to discuss a new deal.
61. **Dues and Subscriptions**: Costs for belonging to professional organizations or trade groups. Example: Paying $200 a year to be a member of the local Chamber of Commerce.
62. **Freight-In**: The shipping cost paid to get inventory into your own warehouse. Example: Paying $50 to a shipping company to deliver a pallet of yarn to your knitting shop.
63. **Freight-Out**: The shipping cost paid to deliver products from your warehouse to customers. Example: Offering "Free Shipping" and paying $10 to deliver a package to a buyer's home.
64. **Equipment Rental**: Costs for using tools or machinery that the business doesn't own. Example: Renting a specialized drill for $100 to complete a one-day construction task.
65. **Cleaning and Janitorial**: Costs to keep the office or workspace clean and sanitary. Example: Paying $300 a month to a cleaning service to vacuum and mop the office floors.
66. **Security Expense**: Costs for guards, alarms, or monitoring systems. Example: A $50 monthly fee for a professional alarm monitoring service.
67. **Commission Expense**: Payments made to sales staff based on the volume of their sales. Example: Giving a salesperson 5% ($50) for closing a $1,000 sale with a new client.
68. **Merchant Account Fees**: The cost of processing credit card payments from customers. Example: Paying $2.9% plus 30 cents on every transaction made through the website's checkout.
69. **Software Amortization**: Gradually recording the cost of a large software purchase as an expense. Example: Buying a $1,200 accounting system and recording a $100 expense every month for a year.
70. **Employee Uniforms**: The cost of providing branded clothing for staff members. Example: Spending $200 to give every new waiter a set of five branded aprons.
71. **Vehicle Fuel and Oil**: Costs to keep business transportation moving. Example: Expensing $60 in gas for a delivery driver's weekly rounds.
72. **Vehicle Insurance**: Specific insurance costs for cars, trucks, or vans used by the business. Example: Paying $500 a year to insure the company's only delivery vehicle.
73. **Audit and Accounting Fees**: Costs for professional reviews of the company's financial records. Example: Paying a CPA firm $2,000 to perform a comprehensive audit of the last three years.
74. **Consulting Fees**: Payments made to outside experts who give advice on how to improve the business. Example: Hiring a specialist for $1,000 to help set up a new inventory management system.
75. **Advertising Production**: Costs to actually create ads, such as photography or graphic design. Example: Paying a photographer $300 to take high-quality pictures for a new product catalog.
76. **Promotional Materials**: Costs for physical giveaways like pens, stickers, or shirts with the company logo. Example: Ordering 500 branded pens for $150 to hand out at a local business fair.
77. **Conference and Seminar Fees**: Costs for employees to attend industry events. Example: Paying $400 for a manager to attend a national retail management conference.
78. **Website Maintenance**: Ongoing costs to keep a company's website updated and online. Example: Paying a developer $100 a month to fix bugs and update content on the company site.
79. **Hosting Fees**: The cost of the server space where a company's website lives. Example: Paying $15 a month for web hosting and domain registration.
80. **Cloud Service Fees**: Costs for online tools like CRM, Project Management, or Email systems. Example: Paying $12 per user per month for a system like Slack or Microsoft Teams.
81. **Franchise Fees**: Ongoing royalties paid by a business owner to a larger parent company. Example: A local burger shop owner paying 5% of all sales to the main corporate office.
82. **Royalties Expense**: Payments made for using someone else's music, images, or ideas. Example: A local radio station paying a small fee every time they play a copyrighted song.
83. **Small Tools and Equipment**: Buying items under a certain price that are expensed immediately rather than depreciated. Example: Buying a $20 hammer for a construction crew and recording it as a current expense.
84. **Printing and Stationery**: Costs for letterheads, business cards, and physical documents. Example: Spending $100 to print 500 professional business cards for a new employee.
85. **Provision for Taxes**: An estimate of the income tax that will be owed at the end of the year. Example: Setting aside $5,000 in the P&L as a "likely" tax bill even if it hasn't been paid yet.
86. **Deferred Income**: Money received for goods not yet delivered, recorded as an obligation. Example: A magazine selling a $120 annual subscription and only recording $10 as revenue each month.
87. **Accrued Salaries**: Wages that have been earned by employees but not yet paid out by the company. Example: Recording a $2,000 expense at the end of the month even if the paychecks are mailed on the 5th.
88. **Accrued Interest**: Interest that has built up on a loan but hasn't been paid yet. Example: Recording a $50 expense for the interest that grew on a loan during the month of June.
89. **Direct Material Costs**: The cost of the actual physical parts that go into a product. Example: The cost of the wood and glue used to build a single chair.
90. **Direct Labor Costs**: The wages paid to people who physically build the product. Example: Paying a carpenter $20 for the hour they spent assembling a specific desk.
91. **Manufacturing Overhead**: All factory-related costs except for direct materials and direct labor. Example: The cost of the electricity used to run the saws in a furniture factory.
92. **Period Costs**: Expenses that are not tied to production and are recorded as they occur. Example: The rent for the main corporate office building, which is independent of factory output.
93. **Product Costs**: Expenses directly tied to creating an item that stay in inventory until sold. Example: The factory workers' wages, which are part of the value of the finished chairs.
94. **Inventory Write-down**: Reducing the recorded value of inventory because its market value has dropped. Example: Lowering the book value of 50 older-model phones because a newer model just launched.
95. **Operating Loss**: Occurs when operating expenses are higher than the gross profit. Example: Making $5,000 in gross profit but spending $7,000 on rent and staff.
96. **Cash Flow vs Profit**: The difference between actual money in the bank and the calculated accounting profit. Example: A business showing a $10,000 profit but having $0 cash because customers haven't paid yet.
97. **Quality Control Costs**: Money spent on inspecting and testing products to ensure they aren't defective. Example: Hiring a staff member to check every 10th item on the assembly line for errors.
98. **Waste and Scrap**: The cost of materials that are ruined or thrown away during production. Example: The value of the extra scraps of leather that are cut away and discarded when making a belt.
99. **Efficiency Variance**: The difference between the planned amount of materials/labor and what was actually used. Example: Finding out it took 2 hours to build a table instead of the budgeted 1 hour.
100. **Balanced P&L Strategy**: Managing revenue and expenses to ensure consistent, sustainable growth over time. Example: Aiming for a steady 15% net profit margin every year rather than aiming for one "giant" month.
