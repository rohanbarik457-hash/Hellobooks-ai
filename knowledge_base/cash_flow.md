# Cash Flow

**Description**: Tracking the movement of money in and out of a business to ensure short-term solvency and operational capacity.

1. **Operating Activities**: Cash flows related to primary business revenue and expenses. Example: Receiving cash from customer payments for services rendered.
2. **Investing Activities**: Cash flows from the purchase and sale of long-term assets. Example: Spending $5000 to buy a new piece of manufacturing equipment.
3. **Financing Activities**: Cash flows related to borrowing money, repaying loans, or owner investments. Example: Receiving $10,000 from a bank loan to expand business operations.
4. **Cash Inflow**: Any transaction that increases the balance of a company's cash accounts. Example: A $200 cash sale made at the register today.
5. **Cash Outflow**: Any transaction that decreases the total cash balance. Example: Paying the monthly $1500 office rent by check.
6. **Net Cash Flow**: The total difference between cash inflows and outflows over a specific period. Example: If inflows are $5000 and outflows are $3000, the net cash flow is $2000.
7. **Cash Equivalents**: Short-term, highly liquid investments that can be converted to cash almost immediately. Example: A 90-day Treasury Bill that is nearing maturity.
8. **Positive Cash Flow**: Occurs when a company's cash inflows exceed its outflows. Example: A business that brings in $10,000 but only spends $8,000 in a month.
9. **Negative Cash Flow**: Occurs when a company's cash outflows exceed its inflows. Example: A startup company spending heavily on marketing while waitng for its first sales.
10. **Free Cash Flow**: The cash left over after a business pays for its operating expenses and capital expenditures. Example: Having $500 left to pay dividends after all bills and new equipment are paid for.
11. **Operating Cash Flow Ratio**: A measure of how well a company can pay its current debts with cash from operations. Example: Having enough cash from sales to cover all immediate supplier bills twice over.
12. **Cash Burn Rate**: The rate at which a new company uses up its initial cash reserves before generating profit. Example: A tech company spending $50,000 more than it makes every month.
13. **Cash Reserve**: A pool of money set aside for emergencies or unexpected business opportunities. Example: Keeping $10,000 in a savings account to cover repairs if the roof leaks.
14. **Direct Method**: A way of reporting cash flows that lists specific sources and uses of cash. Example: Listing exactly how much cash came from "Customers" and went to "Suppliers".
15. **Indirect Method**: A way of reporting cash flows that starts with net income and stays adjusted for non-cash items. Example: Starting with $5000 profit and adding back $1000 in depreciation.
16. **Cash Management**: The process of collecting, managing, and investing physical and electronic cash. Example: Using a specialist service to pick up cash from retail locations daily.
17. **Liquidity Analysis**: Evaluating how easily a company can meet its immediate financial obligations with cash on hand. Example: Checking if you have enough money in the bank to pay salaries tomorrow.
18. **Cash Forecast**: An estimate of future cash inflows and outflows based on historical data. Example: Predicting you will need $2000 extra in December for seasonal staff bonuses.
19. **Solvency**: The long-term ability of a company to pay all its debts and stay in business. Example: Ensuring total assets are significantly higher than total liabilities over many years.
20. **Capital Expenditures (CapEx)**: Money spent by a business to acquire or maintain fixed assets. Example: Buying a commercial refrigerator for a restaurant.
21. **Depreciation (Non-Cash Expense)**: The allocation of the cost of an asset over its useful life; it doesn't involve actual cash movement. Example: Writing off $1000/year for a van, even though no cash leaves the bank that year.
22. **Amortization (Non-Cash Expense)**: The systematic reduction of the carrying amount of an intangible asset. Example: Gradually reducing the book value of a $10,000 patent.
23. **Working Capital**: The difference between current assets and current liabilities. Example: Using $5000 in surplus cash to buy inventory for a holiday sale.
24. **Dividends Paid**: Cash distributed to company shareholders from the business's profits. Example: Sending out quarterly checks of $200 to each of the company's investors.
25. **Interest Collected**: Cash inflow from interest earned on bank accounts or loans made to others. Example: Receiving $20 in monthly interest from a business savings account.
26. **Interest Paid**: Cash outflow from paying interest on borrowed funds or loans. Example: A $50 monthly interest charge on a business credit card balance.
27. **Taxes Paid**: Cash outflow for income, payroll, or sales taxes owed to the government. Example: Making a quarterly $3000 tax payment to the IRS.
28. **Customer Deposits**: Cash received from customers for work that hasn't started yet. Example: A contractor taking a $1000 down payment for a bathroom remodel.
29. **Supplier Payments**: The movement of cash from the business to its vendors for goods or services. Example: Writing a $200 check to the local stationery store for paper.
30. **Payroll Outflow**: The total cash paid to employees, including salaries and benefits. Example: Paying out $8000 in total wages to a team of five people.
31. **Loan Repayment**: The cash outflow used to pay back the principal amount of a debt. Example: Paying $500 toward the balance of a bank loan.
32. **Owner's Draw**: Cash taken out of the business by the owner for personal use. Example: A small business owner taking $1000 to pay their personal house rent.
33. **Proceeds from Asset Sale**: Cash inflow from selling old equipment or property. Example: Selling a five-year-old delivery van for $3000.
34. **Stock Issuance**: Cash inflow from selling shares of the company to new investors. Example: Raising $50,000 by selling 10% of the business to an angel investor.
35. **Treasury Stock Purchase**: Cash outflow when a company buys back its own shares from the market. Example: A large corporation spending $1 million to reduce the number of shares held by the public.
36. **Contingency Fund**: A specific cash reserve for unforeseen "what-if" scenarios. Example: Setting aside $2000 in case the office air conditioner breaks during summer.
37. **Restricted Cash**: Cash held for a specific purpose that cannot be used for daily operations. Example: A $5000 security deposit held in an escrow account.
38. **Cash Collections**: The process of actually receiving money from customers who were billed on credit. Example: Following up with clients whose invoices are 40 days overdue.
39. **Float**: The time between when a check is written and when the money is actually removed from the account. Example: Writing a check on Friday but the bank only processes it on Tuesday.
40. **Petty Cash Replenishment**: The process of adding money back to the office's physical cash box. Example: Putting $50 back into the petty cash tin after employees used it for stamps.
41. **Short-Term Borrowing**: Taking out a small loan or using a line of credit that must be paid back quickly. Example: Withdrawing $500 from a credit line to cover a temporary cash shortage.
42. **Cash Cycle (CCC)**: The time it takes for a company to convert its investments in inventory back into cash. Example: From buying raw materials to receiving the final payment for the finished product.
43. **Inventory Tie-up**: Occurs when too much cash is stuck in unsold products sitting in the warehouse. Example: Having $20,000 worth of winter coats still on shelves in April.
44. **Accounts Receivable Aging**: Tracking how long customer invoices have been unpaid. Example: Seeing that $1000 is 30 days old and $5000 is 90 days old.
45. **Credit Terms Impact**: How the time allowed for customers to pay affects current cash levels. Example: Switching from "Net 60" to "Net 30" to get cash into the business faster.
46. **Disbursement Timing**: Strategically choosing when to pay bills to keep cash in the bank longer. Example: Waiting until the last day of the month to pay a utility bill without a late fee.
47. **Cash Position**: The amount of cash a business has available at a specific moment in time. Example: Checking the online banking app to see if there is currently $1500 available.
48. **Electronic Cash Management**: Using online banking and software to track and move funds. Example: Using a mobile app to deposit a customer's check immediately.
49. **Overdraft Protection**: A bank service that covers checks or transfers when the account balance is zero. Example: The bank automatically lending $100 to cover a $50 overage on a bill.
50. **Lockbox System**: A service where customer payments are sent directly to a bank-controlled mailbox for faster processing. Example: Having clients mail checks to a bank address instead of the office.
51. **Compensating Balance**: A minimum cash balance that a bank requires a company to keep in its account. Example: A bank requiring a $5000 minimum balance to avoid monthly fees on a business loan.
52. **Sweep Accounts**: Accounts that automatically move excess cash into interest-bearing investments at the end of the day. Example: Moving everything over $1000 into a money market fund every night.
53. **Zero Balance Account (ZBA)**: A secondary account used for specific tasks that is automatically refilled by a master account only when needed. Example: A payroll account that only has money in it on payday.
54. **Wire Transfer Outflow**: The immediate electronic movement of cash to another party, often for a fee. Example: Sending $5000 to an international supplier via a bank wire.
55. **Automatic Clearing House (ACH)**: An electronic network used for recurring batched payments. Example: An employee's paycheck being direct-deposited into their bank account every two weeks.
56. **Sales of Marketable Securities**: Turning short-term investments like stocks or bonds back into cash. Example: Selling $2000 worth of company stock to pay for a surprise repair.
57. **Proceeds from Insurance**: Cash inflow received from an insurance company after a claim. Example: Receiving $5000 from insurance after a storm damaged the company warehouse.
58. **Charitable Contributions**: Cash outflow from the business to a non-profit organization. Example: Donating $100 to a local community food bank.
59. **Interest Coverage Ratio**: A measure of how many times a company can pay its interest expenses with its operating cash flow. Example: Making $5000 in profit which easily covers $500 in monthly interest.
60. **Cash ROI (Return on Investment)**: Measuring the cash flow generated by a specific business investment. Example: Buying a $1000 machine that brings in an extra $200 in cash every month.
61. **Financial Flexibility**: The ability of a business to change its cash flows to react to new opportunities. Example: Having enough extra cash to buy a competitor's equipment at an auction.
62. **Seasonality of Cash**: Consistent predictable changes in cash levels during certain times of the year. Example: A toy store having very high cash levels in December and low levels in June.
63. **Cash Flow Volatility**: The degree to which cash inflows and outflows vary wildly from month to month. Example: A freelance consultant who makes $10,000 one month and $0 the next.
64. **Operating Lease Payments**: Cash outflows for renting equipment or space that doesn't result in ownership. Example: Paying $200/month to rent a high-volume office photocopier.
65. **Capital Lease Payments**: Cash outflows for equipment that will eventually be owned by the company. Example: Paying $500/month for a truck with the option to buy it for $1 at the end.
66. **Vendor Credit**: An arrangement where a supplier allows a business to delay cash payment for goods. Example: Buying $1000 of milk for a cafe and not paying until the following Friday.
67. **Factoring Receivables**: Selling unpaid customer invoices to a third party at a discount for immediate cash. Example: Getting $900 today for a $1000 invoice that isn't due for 60 days.
68. **Cash Basis Net Income**: A simplified profit calculation that only looks at cash movements. Example: Subtracting $2000 in cash expenses from $5000 in cash sales.
69. **Non-Operating Cash Flows**: Money in or out from activities not related to core business. Example: Receiving $5000 from the sale of an old office building.
70. **Cash Outflow for Fines**: Money paid to regulatory bodies or agencies due to violations. Example: Paying a $100 parking ticket for a company delivery vehicle.
71. **Cash Flow per Share**: The total cash flow from operations divided by the number of company shares. Example: Generating $100,000 in cash for a company with 10,000 shares ($10 per share).
72. **Bridge Loan Inflow**: A short-term loan taken out to provide immediate cash while waiting for long-term financing. Example: Borrowing $5000 for two weeks until a major client pays their bill.
73. **Accrued Expense Payment**: Cash outflow for an expense that was previously recorded as a liability. Example: Paying the $2000 salary that was recorded in the books last month.
74. **Customer Credit Limit**: The maximum amount of credit a business will extend to a client before requiring cash. Example: Telling a client they must pay cash for any orders over $5000.
75. **Cash Concentration**: Consolidating cash from multiple smaller bank accounts into one main central account. Example: An owner of 5 gas stations moving all daily profits into one business account.
76. **In-Store Credit Outflow**: Providing a refund to a customer in the form of a voucher instead of physical cash. Example: Giving a customer a $50 gift card for a returned shirt.
77. **Cash Discount Term: 2/10**: A specific term meaning a 2% discount is given if paid within 10 days. Example: Settling a $1000 invoice for $980 because it was paid early.
78. **Cash Disbursement Journal**: A manual or digital log of every single check or cash payment made. Example: A book listing every payment made to vendors, landlords, and staff this month.
79. **Cash Sufficiency**: The state of having enough cash to cover basic operational needs without borrowing. Example: A profitable restaurant that never needs to use a credit card for supplies.
80. **Projected Cash Shortage**: A warning from a forecast that the bank balance will drop below zero soon. Example: Realizing you will be $1000 short for payroll next month unless you sell more.
81. **Unchecked Cash Leakage**: Small, unrecognized cash outflows that add up to major losses over time. Example: Employees using the business card for small personal snacks or gas.
82. **Cash Inflow from Grants**: Money received from the government or organizations that doesn't need to be repaid. Example: A small business receiving a $2000 grant for going "green".
83. **Maturity of Cash Equivalents**: The date when an investment like a CD becomes available as actual cash. Example: A 6-month bond ending and the money appearing in the business checking account.
84. **Bank Reconciliation: Error Correction**: Adjusting cash records when a mistake is found on the bank statement. Example: Fixing a $20 error where the bank accidentally charged a fee twice.
85. **Dividend Reinvestment Outflow**: Using profit to buy more company shares instead of taking cash out. Example: An investor choosing to get "extra shares" instead of a $100 check.
86. **Cash Flow Sensitivity**: How much a business's cash levels change when sales or costs change slightly. Example: A high-sensitivity business where a 5% drop in sales causes a 50% drop in cash.
87. **Pre-Authorized Debit (PAD)**: An arrangement where a company allows a vendor to pull cash from their account. Example: Allowing the electricity company to automatically take $200 every month.
88. **Cash Inflow from Royalties**: Money received for allowing others to use your intellectual property. Example: An author receiving $500 in cash for every 1000 books sold.
89. **Cash Outflow for Licenses**: Money paid to the government for the right to operate a business. Example: Paying a yearly $50 "Business Operating License" fee.
90. **Currency Exchange Impact**: How changes in global money values affect the cash levels of international businesses. Example: Losing $50 in cash value because the exchange rate changed during a slow transfer.
91. **Cash Dividend Declaration**: A formal announcement that a cash payment will be made to shareholders. Example: A board meeting where it's decided that $0.50 per share will be paid in cash.
92. **Cumulative Cash Flow**: The total amount of cash generated from the beginning of a project or business. Example: Tracking that a new machine has brought in a total of $5000 over three years.
93. **Cash Inflow from Settlements**: Money received after a legal dispute is resolved. Example: Receiving $2000 after winning a small claims court case against a vendor.
94. **Cash Outflow for Refunds**: Returning money to a customer after they cancel a contract or return a product. Example: Handing back $50 to a customer who changed their mind about a purchase.
95. **Bank Reconciliation: Interest Earned**: Adding money to the cash records that the bank statement shows was earned. Example: Noticing the bank added $2.45 in interest and recording it in the ledger.
96. **Cash Flow Sustainability**: The ability of a business to maintain a positive cash flow over many years. Example: A business that consistently makes more than it spends without needing new loans.
97. **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**: A calculation used to determine the profitability of potential cash-generating investments. Example: Comparing two machines to see which one brings in the most cash over time.
98. **Net Present Value (NPV)**: Adjusting future cash flows to show what they are worth in today's money. Example: Knowing that $1000 next year is worth less than $1000 today due to inflation.
99. **Cash Squeeze**: A difficult period where cash is very tight but the business is still technically profitable. Example: Having $50,000 in expected customer payments but only $50 in the bank today.
100. **Balanced Cash Strategy**: Maintaining a healthy mix of cash reserves, investments, and debt payments. Example: Splitting monthly profit between a' rainy day fund' and paying off old loans.
