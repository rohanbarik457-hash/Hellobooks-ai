# Invoices

**Description**: Detailed documentation of sales transactions, payment terms, and billing information for business-to-business and consumer transactions.

1. **Invoicing**: The process of generating and sending a bill to a customer for products or services provided. Example: Sending a PDF invoice to a client after completing their website design.
2. **Billing Terms**: Specific conditions regarding how and when a customer should pay for their purchase. Example: Specifying "Net 15" on an invoice, meaning payment is due within 15 days.
3. **Itemized Billing**: Listing each product or service separately on an invoice with its individual price. Example: An invoice showing "Consulting - $100" and "Travel Expense - $20" on separate lines.
4. **Due Date**: The specific calendar date by which an invoice must be paid to avoid late fees. Example: A bill sent on June 1st with a due date of June 30th.
5. **Discount Handling**: Applying price reductions to an invoice for early payment or special promotions. Example: Taking 5% off the total price because the customer used a holiday coupon code.
6. **Payment Reminders**: Automated or manual messages sent to customers when an invoice is approaching its due date. Example: Sending a friendly email three days before a large payment is due.
7. **Pro-forma Invoice**: A preliminary bill sent to a buyer before shipment, often for customs or financing purposes. Example: Sending an estimated bill so a client can get approval from their manager.
8. **Commercial Invoice**: A document used for international trade that describes the goods being shipped and their value. Example: An invoice used by customs officials to determine how much tax to charge on imported shoes.
9. **Recurring Invoicing**: Automatically sending the same bill to a customer at regular intervals. Example: A monthly software subscription where the user is billed $19.99 on the 1st of every month.
10. **Late Fee Application**: Adding an extra charge to an invoice when it is not paid by the due date. Example: Adding a $10 fee to a utility bill that is 10 days past due.
11. **Credit Note (Credit Memo)**: A document issued to a customer to reduce the amount they owe, often for returns. Example: Removing $50 from a bill after a customer returns a defective toaster.
12. **Debit Note (Debit Memo)**: A document issued to increase the amount a customer owes due to an undercharge or extra services. Example: Adding $15 to an invoice because the shipping weight was higher than estimated.
13. **Invoice Status Tracking**: Monitoring whether an invoice is drafted, sent, viewed, or paid. Example: Checking an accounting dashboard to see that "Invoice #101" was opened by the client.
14. **Billing Address**: The specific physical or digital location where a customer's invoices should be sent. Example: Sending an electronic bill to "billing@examplecompany.com".
15. **Shipping Address**: The physical location where the actual products are delivered, which may be different from the billing address. Example: Mailing a laptop to "123 Tech Road" while billing the company headquarters.
16. **Currency Specification**: Identifying which type of money is being used for the invoice totals. Example: Explicitly stating that the total is "500 USD" to avoid confusion with other currencies.
17. **Purchase Order (PO) Number**: A reference number provided by the buyer to track the purchase in their own system. Example: A client asking you to put "PO #889" on the invoice so their finance team knows which project it is for.
18. **Tax Calculation**: Automatically adding sales tax, VAT, or GST to the invoice total based on location. Example: Adding 8% sales tax to a $100 purchase for a total of $108.
19. **Unit Price**: The cost of a single instance of a product or one hour of service. Example: Charging $25 per hour for yard work.
20. **Quantity (Qty)**: The total number of items or units of service being billed. Example: Sending an invoice for "5 Printers" where each printer costs $200.
21. **Sales Tax ID**: A unique number listed on an invoice to show the business is registered with the government. Example: Including a VAT number on a European invoice so the buyer can claim a tax credit.
22. **Subtotal**: The total cost of all items on an invoice before taxes or discounts are added. Example: Three $10 items resulting in a subtotal of $30.
23. **Balance Due**: The remaining amount a customer owes on an invoice after any partial payments. Example: A $500 invoice that has had $200 paid, leaving $300 as the balance due.
24. **Past Due Invoice**: An invoice where the payment date has passed without the full balance being settled. Example: A bill that was due on Monday but is still unpaid on Friday.
25. **Billing Cycle**: The frequency at which invoices are generated (e.g., monthly, quarterly). Example: A landlord who bills for office space on the 1st of every month.
26. **Net 30**: A common payment term meaning the customer has 30 days from the invoice date to pay. Example: An invoice dated May 1st that is due on May 31st.
27. **2/10 Net 30**: Offering a 2% discount if paid in 10 days, otherwise the full amount is due in 30 days. Example: A customer paying $980 on a $1000 bill because they paid on day 5.
28. **E-Invoicing**: The electronic exchange of an invoice document between a supplier and a buyer. Example: A computer system automatically sending an XML file to a client's accounting software.
29. **Disputed Invoice**: An invoice that the customer refuses to pay due to an error or disagreement over service quality. Example: A client refusing to pay for "extra hours" that they say they did not authorize.
30. **Statement of Account**: A summary report sent to a customer showing all their open invoices and total balance. Example: Sending a list of 5 unpaid bills to a client to remind them of their total $5000 debt.
31. **Early Payment Discount**: A financial incentive offered to encourage customers to pay before the due date. Example: Giving a client $10 off if they pay their bill immediately upon receipt.
32. **Progress Billing (Milestone Billing)**: Invoicing for portions of a project as they are completed. Example: A contractor billing 25% after the foundation is poured and another 25% after the walls are up.
33. **Retainer Invoice**: A bill sent in advance to secure future services or cover upcoming expenses. Example: A lawyer charging $1000 upfront before starting to work on a case.
34. **Catch-up Billing**: An invoice sent to cover costs that were missed in previous billing cycles. Example: Billing for three months of electricity after the meter wasn't read for a long time.
35. **Grace Period**: An extra amount of time after the due date during which no late fees are charged. Example: A bill due on the 10th that allows payment until the 15th without penalty.
36. **Interim Invoice**: A small bill sent in the middle of a large project for completed tasks. Example: Billing for the "logo design" part of a larger "branding" project.
37. **Consolidated Invoicing**: Combining multiple smaller orders into one single monthly bill. Example: An office supply company sending one big invoice for all the pens and paper bought in June.
38. **Automatic Payments (Auto-pay)**: A system where a customer's credit card or bank is automatically charged on the due date. Example: Your internet bill being deducted from your checking account every month on the 5th.
39. **Billing Error**: A mistake on an invoice, such as an incorrect price, quantity, or tax rate. Example: Accidentally charging for 100 items when only 10 were delivered.
40. **Write-off**: When a business decides an unpaid invoice will never be collected and removes it from the books. Example: Canceling a $50 debt because the customer has moved out of the country and can't be found.
41. **Factoring Invoices**: Selling your unpaid invoices to a third party to get cash immediately. Example: Getting $900 today from a company that will wait 60 days to collect $1000 from your client.
42. **Remittance Advice**: A document sent by a buyer to a seller to show that an invoice has been paid. Example: A client emailing a "Payment Confirmation" slip along with their check.
43. **Self-Billing**: When the buyer creates the invoice themselves and sends it to the supplier for approval. Example: A large supermarket chain generating bills for the farmers based on the weight of vegetables received.
44. **Time and Materials (T&M) Billing**: Charging based on the actual hours worked and the cost of supplies used. Example: A plumber charging $80/hour plus the cost of the new pipes replace.
45. **Flat-Rate Billing**: A fixed price for a specific service regardless of how long it takes. Example: Charging a standard $150 for an oil change and tire rotation.
46. **Value-Based Billing**: Setting prices based on the perceived value to the customer rather than the cost of production. Example: An agency charging $10,000 for a logo that will be used on millions of products.
47. **Deposit Requirement**: Requesting a portion of the total price before any work begins or goods are shipped. Example: A custom furniture maker asking for $500 before starting to build a table.
48. **Collection Agency**: A third-party company hired to recover money from very overdue invoices. Example: Hiring a firm to call a customer who hasn't paid a $5000 bill for over six months.
49. **Invoice Aging Report**: An internal report that categorizes unpaid bills by how many days they are past due. Example: A report showing that $3000 in bills are 1-30 days old and $1000 are 90+ days old.
50. **Payment Gateway**: The technology that processes credit card or debit payments for electronic invoices. Example: Clicking a "Pay Now" button on an invoice that takes the client to a secure Stripe portal.
51. **Rounding Rules**: Specific policies on how to handle decimal points and cents on an invoice. Example: A company that rounds all totals to the nearest whole dollar for simplicity.
52. **Reference Number**: A unique string used to identify a specific transaction in a database. Example: Telling a customer service agent that your "Customer ID" is 9928-X.
53. **Voided Invoice**: An invoice that was created in error and cancelled before it was finalized or sent. Example: Deleting a draft bill that was accidentally created for the wrong client.
54. **Duplicate Invoice**: An error where the same transaction is billed twice to the same customer. Example: Sending two $500 bills for the same office repair by mistake.
55. **Billing Inquiries**: Questions from customers regarding specific charges or terms on an invoice. Example: A customer calling to ask why they were charged a shipping fee when their order was over $100.
56. **Merchant Fees**: The cost a business pays to process a customer's credit card payment. Example: Paying $2.90 to the bank for every $100 a customer pays via Visa.
57. **Overdue Interest**: A percentage-based fee added to a bill for every month it remains unpaid. Example: Charging 1.5% interest every month on a $1000 balance that is late.
58. **Subscription Tiers**: Different billing levels for different levels of service. Example: A "Basic" plan at $10/month and a "Premium" plan at $30/month.
59. **Prorated Billing**: Charging a customer only for the portion of the billing cycle they actually used. Example: Charging $15 for half a month of service if the customer joined on the 15th.
60. **Annual Billing Discount**: Offering a lower total price if the customer pays for a whole year at once. Example: Charging $100 for a year instead of $10 per month (saving the user $20).
61. **Third-Party Billing**: When an invoice is sent to a person or company other than the one who received the service. Example: A student seeing a doctor, but the bill is sent directly to their insurance company.
62. **Bill-of-Lading**: A document between a shipper and a carrier that lists the goods being transported. Example: A truck driver signing a paper saying they have received 50 boxes of electronics to deliver.
63. **Cash on Delivery (COD)**: A payment term where the customer pays the delivery person at the time they receive the goods. Example: Paying for a pizza in cash when the driver arrives at your door.
64. **Advance Payment**: Paying for an order in full before it is processed or shipped. Example: Buying a concert ticket online two months before the actual show.
65. **Standard Payment Terms**: The default rules a business uses for all its regular customers. Example: Every new client at a law firm is told that bills are "Due on Receipt".
66. **Service Level Agreement (SLA) Penalties**: Reductions in an invoice total if a company fails to meet its agreed-upon performance goals. Example: Giving a $50 credit because the internet was down for two days.
67. **Expense Reimbursement**: Adding the cost of travel or supplies to a client's invoice for repayment. Example: Billing a client for the $40 train ticket used to visit their office.
68. **Non-Taxable Items**: Products or services that are exempt from sales tax based on local laws. Example: Most groceries and prescriptions being sold without adding additional tax in many regions.
69. **Tax-Exempt Customers**: Organizations like charities or schools that are not required to pay sales tax. Example: Not charging tax on a $500 supply order for a local registered non-profit.
70. **Billing Dispute Resolution**: The process of fixing an invoice after a customer complains about an error. Example: Refunding a $20 "overcharge" after a client proved they were quoted a lower price.
71. **Late Payment Penalties**: Specific one-time fines added to a bill immediately after the due date. Example: A landlord charging a $50 "late fee" if the rent is not paid by the 5th.
72. **Payment Allocations**: Deciding which unpaid bill a customer's payment should be applied to first. Example: Using a $500 check to pay off the "oldest" bill first.
73. **Under-billing**: An error where a business forgets to charge for all the goods or services provided. Example: Only billing for 8 hours of work when 10 hours were actually completed.
74. **Over-billing**: An error where a business accidentally charges more than the agreed price. Example: Charging $500 for a repair that was supposed to cost only $450.
75. **Credit Limit Check**: Verifying a customer's payment history before allowing them to buy more on credit. Example: Refusing to ship a new order because the client already owes $10,000 and is late.
76. **Manual Invoicing**: Creating a bill using paper forms or basic word processing instead of accounting software. Example: Writing out a handwritten receipt for a cash sale at a flea market.
77. **Batch Invoicing**: Generating hundreds or thousands of invoices at the same time using an automated system. Example: A multi-unit apartment building printing all rent bills at once on the 25th.
78. **Invoice Matching (3-way match)**: Comparing the invoice to the purchase order and the receiving report to ensure accuracy. Example: Checking that the "bill" matches the "order" and the "box" that arrived.
79. **Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT)**: Moving money from a buyer's bank to a seller's bank electronically. Example: A client paying their monthly bill via a direct bank-to-bank wire transfer.
80. **Billing Department**: The specific team in a company responsible for managing accounts receivable and sending bills. Example: Calling "Extension 4" to talk to someone about an error on your invoice.
81. **Invoice Header**: The top part of an invoice containing the company logo, name, and contact details. Example: Seeing the company address and phone number at the very top of the page.
82. **Invoice Footer**: The bottom part of an invoice containing payment instructions and legal notes. Example: Reading at the bottom that "All checks should be made payable to ABC Corp".
83. **Billable Hours**: The specific time spent by an employee that can be charged directly to a client. Example: An accountant spending 3 hours on a client's tax return and billing for exactly that time.
84. **Non-Billable Hours**: Time spent on a project that cannot be charged to the client, such as training or internal meetings. Example: A designer spending an hour learning new software which they don't charge to any client.
85. **Partial Payment**: When a customer pays only a portion of the total amount due on an invoice. Example: A customer paying $100 toward a $500 bill because they had an unexpected emergency.
86. **Pre-bill**: An invoice sent before work is done, often used in professional services. Example: An architect billing for $2000 before they start drawing the final blueprints.
87. **Back-billing**: Charging a customer for services provided in the past that were never invoiced. Example: Billing for a year-old consultation that was accidentally left out of the monthly reports.
88. **Billing Period**: The specific range of dates covered by a particular invoice. Example: A mobile phone bill covering usage from "Aug 1st to Aug 31st".
89. **Invoice Reconciliation**: The process of matching payments received to the specific invoices they were meant for. Example: Figuring out that a $300 check was for "Invoice #5" and "Invoice #8".
90. **Remittance Address**: The specific location where paper checks or physical payments should be mailed. Example: The "P.O. Box 500" address listed on the payment stub of a bill.
91. **Sales Commission Invoicing**: A bill sent to a company by a salesperson or agent for their share of a sale. Example: An agent sending an invoice for 10% of the $5000 deal they just closed.
92. **Early Settlement Discount**: Another term for a discount given when an invoice is paid early. Example: Saving $20 on a $1000 bill because it was paid 20 days before the deadline.
93. **Invoice Verification**: The internal process of checking a bill for errors before it is sent to the customer. Example: A manager reviewing all draft invoices on Friday afternoon to look for mistakes.
94. **Billing Software**: Computer programs designed specifically to create, send, and track business invoices. Example: Using FreshBooks or QuickBooks to manage all company billing.
95. **Invoice Template**: A pre-designed form that a business uses to ensure all bills look consistent and professional. Example: Having a "Standard Invoice" file that already has the logo and bank details saved.
96. **Inbound Invoicing**: The process of receiving and recording bills from your own suppliers. Example: Logging the $200 electricity bill that arrived in today's mail as an "Account Payable".
97. **Outbound Invoicing**: The process of creating and sending bills to your own customers. Example: Exporting 50 PDF bills from the system to send to clients via email.
98. **Direct Debit**: A payment method where a business is authorized to take money directly from a customer's bank account. Example: A gym membership that pulls $40 from your account on the 1st of every month automatically.
99. **Invoice Numbering System**: A logical way of assigning ID numbers to invoices (e.g., sequential or date-based). Example: Starting the first invoice of the year as 2024-001.
100. **Balanced Invoicing**: Ensuring that every charge on a bill is accurately reflected in the company's revenue accounts. Example: Checking that the $50 on "Invoice #10" matches the $50 recorded in the "Sales" ledger.
