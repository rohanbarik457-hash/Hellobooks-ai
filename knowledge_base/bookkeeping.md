# Bookkeeping

**Description**: Fundamental principles and practices of recording daily financial transactions for accurate business tracking.

1. **Recording Transactions**: Systematically logging every financial exchange, including purchases, sales, receipts, and payments. Example: Recording a $50 office supply purchase in the accounting software immediately.
2. **Double-Entry Accounting**: A system where every transaction affects at least two accounts, ensuring the accounting equation remains in balance. Example: When cash increases by $1000 due to a sale, Revenue also increases by $1000.
3. **General Ledger**: The master record of all financial accounts, providing a comprehensive history of a company's financial transactions. Example: Looking up the "Rent Expense" account in the ledger to see all payments made this year.
4. **Journal Entries**: The initial logs of individual transactions, detailing dates, amounts, and a brief description. Example: Creating an entry on Jan 1st for "Utility Bill Payment - $200".
5. **Accounts Payable**: The record of money a company owes to its suppliers or vendors for goods and services received on credit. Example: A $500 invoice from a paper supplier that is due in 30 days.
6. **Accounts Receivable**: The tracking of money owed to a company by its customers for goods or services delivered but not yet paid for. Example: A client owes $1200 for a consulting project completed last week.
7. **Reconciliation**: Comparing internal financial records with external statements (like bank statements) to ensure consistency. Example: Matching the ending balance in the checking account with the bank's monthly statement.
8. **Trial Balance**: A report listing the balances of all general ledger accounts to verify that total debits equal total credits. Example: Running a report at months-end to check for mathematical errors in the ledger.
9. **Financial Record Accuracy**: Maintaining precise logs to ensure reliable reporting and informed decision-making. Example: Double-checking data entry to prevent a $10.00 charge from being recorded as $100.
10. **Expense Tracking**: Monitoring outgoing funds to manage cash flow and identify areas for cost reduction. Example: Using a spreadsheet to categorize all monthly subscriptions and utility costs.
11. **Revenue Recording**: Documenting incoming funds from primary business activities to evaluate profitability. Example: Entering total daily sales from a retail store's point-of-sale system.
12. **Chart of Accounts**: An organized list of all financial accounts used by a business, categorized into types. Example: Setting up account code 101 for Cash and 401 for Sales Revenue.
13. **Audit Preparation**: Keeping chronological and organized records with supporting documents to facilitate examinations. Example: Filing all physical receipts in monthly folders to be ready for a tax review.
14. **Daily Transaction Logging**: The routine practice of entering financial data promptly to prevent backlog. Example: Spending 10 minutes every evening entering the day's invoices.
15. **Tax Documentation**: Compiling and retaining necessary financial records and receipts required for accurate filing. Example: Keeping 1099 forms and payroll records for 7 years as required by law.
16. **Asset Tracking**: Recording the acquisition, depreciation, and disposal of company-owned resources. Example: Logging the purchase date and cost of a new computer to calculate its yearly value loss.
17. **Liability Tracking**: Monitoring ongoing financial obligations, loan balances, and accrued expenses. Example: Tracking the remaining balance on a business loan after each monthly payment.
18. **Financial Transparency**: Clear accounting practices that provide stakeholders with a truthful view of health. Example: Sharing monthly profit reports with business partners honestly.
19. **Internal Control Practices**: Implementing checks and balances, such as segregating duties, to prevent fraud. Example: Ensuring the person who writes checks is not the same person who reconciles the bank statement.
20. **Year-End Closing**: Finalizing records for a fiscal year and carrying forward permanent balances. Example: Closing out the "Sales" account to zero to start fresh for the next year.
21. **Accrual Basis Accounting**: Recording revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of cash flow. Example: Recording a sale in December even if the customer doesn't pay until January.
22. **Cash Basis Accounting**: Recording revenue and expenses only when cash actually changes hands. Example: Not recording an expense until the check is actually mailed and cashed.
23. **Petty Cash Management**: Maintaining a small amount of physical cash for minor office expenses. Example: Using $5 from a locked drawer to buy stamps for the office.
24. **Bank Deposit Tracking**: Recording the details of every physical or electronic deposit made into business accounts. Example: Matching a $2000 deposit slip to a specific set of customer invoices.
25. **Payroll Processing**: Calculating and recording employee wages, taxes, and benefits. Example: Calculating the net pay for an employee after deducting federal and state taxes.
26. **Inventory Valuation**: Determining the cost of goods remaining on hand at the end of a period. Example: Using the FIFO method to value remaining stock based on the oldest purchase prices.
27. **Fixed Asset Register**: A detailed list of all long-term assets including their location and cost. Example: Maintaining a list of all company vehicles with their VIN numbers and purchase prices.
28. **Depreciation Schedule**: A table showing the systematic reduction in the value of an asset over time. Example: Recording a $1000 "wear and tear" expense every year for a $5000 machine.
29. **Credit Memo Processing**: Recording a reduction in the amount a customer owes due to returned goods. Example: Issuing a $50 credit to a customer who returned a defective product.
30. **Debit Memo Processing**: Recording an increase in the amount owed to a supplier for additional charges. Example: Adjusting a supplier balance after they corrected an undercharged invoice.
31. **Voucher System**: An internal control where every payment requires a formal authorized document. Example: Not paying an invoice until a manager signs a "Payment Voucher" attached to the receipt.
32. **Subsidiary Ledgers**: Detailed records that support a specific general ledger account. Example: An "Accounts Receivable Ledger" that lists exactly how much each individual customer owes.
33. **Bad Debt Expense**: Estimating and recording the portion of accounts receivable that won't be collected. Example: Writing off $200 because a customer went out of business and can't pay.
34. **Prepaid Expense Amortization**: Gradually recording a previously paid expense over the period it benefits. Example: Paying $1200 for annual insurance and recording $100 as an expense each month.
35. **Unearned Revenue**: Money received from a customer before the work has been performed. Example: Receiving a $500 deposit for a service to be delivered next month.
36. **Contingent Liabilities**: Potential obligations that depend on the outcome of a future event. Example: Recording a note about a pending lawsuit that might cost the company money.
37. **Proprietorship Accounts**: Capital and withdrawal accounts used in businesses owned by one person. Example: Recording "John Doe, Capital" to show how much the owner has invested.
38. **Partnership Accounting**: Managing separate capital and drawing accounts for multiple business owners. Example: Distributing yearly profits 50/50 between two business partners.
39. **Retained Earnings Statement**: A report showing how much profit has been kept in the company over time. Example: Adding this year's $10,000 profit to the previous $50,000 in retained earnings.
40. **Dividends Payable**: Profits that have been declared by a corporation to be paid to shareholders. Example: A board of directors voting to pay out $1 per share to all investors.
41. **Standard Costing**: Assigning an "expected" cost to products rather than the actual cost for better analysis. Example: Budgeting $5 per unit for raw materials and comparing it to the actual $5.20 cost.
42. **Variance Analysis**: Comparing actual accounting results to budgeted figures to find differences. Example: Investigating why utility costs were $500 over the monthly budget.
43. **Break-Even Analysis**: Calculating the point where total revenue equals total expenses. Example: Determining that the company needs to sell 100 widgets to cover all fixed costs.
44. **Margin of Safety**: The difference between actual sales and the break-even sales point. Example: Knowing the company can handle a 10% drop in sales before losing money.
45. **Cost Volume Profit (CVP)**: Studying how changes in costs and volume affect a company's profit. Example: Calculating if lowering prices will lead to enough sales volume to increase total profit.
46. **Job Order Costing**: Tracking costs for specific unique "jobs" or projects. Example: A construction company tracking labor and materials specifically for "Building A".
47. **Process Costing**: Assigning costs to mass-produced identical units. Example: A chemical factory calculating the average cost per gallon for a large batch of paint.
48. **Activity Based Costing (ABC)**: Allocating overhead costs based on specific activities that drive expenses. Example: Allocating machine maintenance costs based on the number of hours each product uses the machines.
49. **Absorption Costing**: Including all manufacturing costs (fixed and variable) in the cost of a product. Example: Adding factory rent and utility costs into the inventory value of every item produced.
50. **Variable Costing**: Only including variable manufacturing costs in the inventory value. Example: Excluding the factory manager's salary from the cost of goods sold.
51. **FOB Shipping Point**: Terms where the buyer pays for shipping and takes ownership at the seller's dock. Example: A company buying supplies from abroad becomes responsible for them as soon as they are loaded on the ship.
52. **FOB Destination**: Terms where the seller pays for shipping and keeps ownership until the goods reach the buyer. Example: A supplier replacing a package that was damaged during delivery before it reached the client's office.
53. **Purchase Returns and Allowances**: Returning defective goods to a supplier for a refund. Example: Sending back 10 broken chairs and receiving a $500 credit on the account.
54. **Sales Discounts**: Offering a price reduction to customers for paying their invoices early. Example: Giving a "2/10, n/30" discount where the customer gets 2% off if they pay within 10 days.
55. **Gross Profit Percentage**: The ratio of gross profit to net sales revenue. Example: Making $40 profit on a $100 sale results in a 40% gross profit margin.
56. **Inventory Turnover Ratio**: How many times a company's inventory is sold and replaced over a period. Example: A high ratio suggesting that products are selling quickly and not gathering dust.
57. **Days Sales in Inventory**: The average number of days it takes to sell the entire inventory. Example: Knowing that on average, a product sits on the shelf for 45 days before being sold.
58. **Current Ratio**: Current Assets divided by Current Liabilities to measure liquidity. Example: Having $2000 in cash to cover $1000 in bills gives a current ratio of 2.0.
59. **Acid Test Ratio**: A stricter liquidity measure that excludes inventory from current assets. Example: Checking if cash and receivables alone can cover immediate debts.
60. **Debt to Equity Ratio**: Total Liabilities divided by Total Equity to measure financial leverage. Example: A ratio of 1.5 meaning the company has $1.50 in debt for every $1.00 of owner investment.
61. **Periodic Inventory System**: Updating inventory records only at the end of an accounting period. Example: Counting all items in the warehouse on December 31st to calculate the year-end value.
62. **Perpetual Inventory System**: Updating inventory records constantly after every sale or purchase. Example: A barcode scanner immediately reducing the stock count in the database when an item is sold.
63. **LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)**: Assuming the newest items purchased are the first ones sold. Example: During rising prices, this method results in a higher cost of goods sold and lower taxes.
64. **FIFO (First-In, First-Out)**: Assuming the oldest items purchased are the first ones sold. Example: Selling the oldest milk in a grocery store first to prevent spoilage.
65. **Weighted Average Cost**: Calculating a single average cost for all items in inventory. Example: If you buy one item at $10 and another at $12, the average cost for both is $11.
66. **LCM (Lower of Cost or Market)**: Valuing inventory at whichever is lower: its original cost or current market value. Example: Reducing the value of high-tech gadgets if a newer model makes the old ones worth less.
67. **Bank Reconciliation: Outstanding Checks**: Checks that have been written but not yet cleared by the bank. Example: You wrote a $500 check on the 30th, but the bank statement shows it hasn't been cashed yet.
68. **Bank Reconciliation: Deposits in Transit**: Money recorded by the business but not yet processed by the bank. Example: A $1000 deposit made in the ATM late at night on the last day of the month.
69: **NSF Checks (Non-Sufficient Funds)**: Checks received from customers that the bank returned because they didn't have enough money. Example: Charging a customer a $35 fee after their $200 payment bounced.
70. **Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT)**: Moving money between bank accounts electronically without paper checks. Example: Paying the monthly office rent through a direct bank-to-bank transfer.
71. **Bank Service Charges**: Fees charged by the bank for maintaining an account or processing wire transfers. Example: Recording a $15 monthly "Account Maintenance Fee" as an expense.
72. **Correcting Entries**: Adjustments made to fix errors found in the accounting records. Example: Moving a $100 charge from "Travel Expense" to "Entertainment" because it was categorized incorrectly.
73. **Adjusting Entries**: Entries made at the end of a period to bring accounts up to date. Example: Recording interest earned on a savings account that wasn't previously logged.
74. **Closing Entries**: Temporary entries that clear out income and expense accounts at year-end. Example: Transferring the total "Electricity Expense" balance to the "Income Summary" account.
75. **Post-Closing Trial Balance**: A trial balance prepared after closing entries to ensure only permanent accounts remain. Example: Verifying that all "Income" and "Expense" accounts now have a zero balance.
76. **Working Capital**: The capital available for day-to-day operations (Current Assets - Current Liabilities). Example: Using $5000 in extra cash to buy more inventory for a busy season.
77. **Solvency**: The ability of a company to meet its long-term financial obligations. Example: Having enough long-term assets to eventually pay off a 10-year mortgage.
78. **Liquidity**: The ability of a company to convert assets into cash quickly to pay short-term bills. Example: Keeping enough money in a savings account to pay employees next Friday.
79. **Going Concern Assumption**: The premise that a business will stay in operation for the foreseeable future. Example: Recording long-term assets at their full cost rather than "garage sale" prices.
80. **Monetary Unit Assumption**: The practice of recording only transactions that can be expressed in money terms. Example: You can't record "happy employees" on a balance sheet, only their salaries in dollars.
81. **Economic Entity Assumption**: Keeping a business's transactions separate from the owner's personal finances. Example: Not paying for the owner's personal groceries with a business credit card.
82. **Time Period Assumption**: Dividing the life of a business into distinct periods for reporting. Example: Reporting profits separately for "Quarter 1" and "Quarter 2".
83. **Revenue Recognition Principle**: Recording revenue in the period it is earned, not necessarily when cash is received. Example: An electrician recording a $300 sale as soon as they finish the job.
84. **Expense Recognition (Matching) Principle**: Recording expenses in the same period as the revenue they helped generate. Example: Recording the cost of a car in the same month you made the profit from selling it.
85. **Full Disclosure Principle**: Providing all necessary information for users to understand financial statements. Example: Adding a note to the financial reports about a pending multi-million dollar lawsuit.
86. **Materiality**: Relates to whether an error or omission is large enough to affect a decision. Example: Rounding cents to the nearest dollar in a $10 million report is "immaterial".
87. **Conservatism Principle**: Choosing the accounting method that is least likely to overstate assets or income. Example: Recording a potential loss as soon as it's likely, but waiting to record a gain until it actually happens.
88. **Consistency Principle**: Using the same accounting methods from one period to the next. Example: If you use "FIFO" this year, you should use "FIFO" next year too for fair comparison.
89. **Cost Principle**: Assets should be recorded at their actual historical cost. Example: Even if a building is worth $1 million now, keeping it at its $500,000 purchase price on the books.
90. **Business Papers / Source Documents**: Physical evidence of a financial transaction. Example: A printed cash register receipt or a signed contract for services.
91. **Credit Sales**: Sales where the customer is allowed to pay at a later date. Example: Sending an invoice with "Net 30" terms allowing the buyer 30 days to pay.
92. **Credit Terms**: The conditions under which a seller will grant credit to a buyer. Example: Specifying that a 5% late fee will be added if a bill isn't paid within 60 days.
93. **Sales Returns**: When a customer brings back a product and receives a full refund. Example: A retail store giving $100 back to a customer who returned an unopened blender.
94. **Sales Allowances**: When a customer keeps a damaged product but receives a partial refund. Example: Giving a client $20 off an invoice because the box was slightly crushed during shipping.
95. **Purchase Discounts**: Reductions in the cost of assets for paying supplier invoices early. Example: Saving $10 on a $500 supply order because you paid within 5 days.
96. **General Journal**: The "book of original entry" where transactions are first recorded. Example: Writing down a chronological list of every sale and expense that happened today.
97. **Ledger Account**: A specific record for each type of asset, liability, equity, revenue, and expense. Example: The page in the book dedicated entirely to tracking "Electricity Bills".
98. **Double-Entry Equality**: The rule that the sum of debits must always equal the sum of credits. Example: If you debit "Cash" for $100, you must credit something else (like "Revenue") for exactly $100.
99. **Accounting Cycle**: The sequence of steps starting with a transaction and ending with financial statements. Example: Step 1 is the transaction, Step 10 is the post-closing trial balance.
100. **Balanced Accounts**: When the total increase in an account is correctly recorded alongside its corresponding decrease. Example: Ensuring every dollar "in" is accounted for by a reason why it came in.
