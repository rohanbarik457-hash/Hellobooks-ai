# Balance Sheet

**Description**: A financial snapshot of a company's position at a specific point in time, detailing its assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity.

1. **Assets**: Anything of tangible or intangible value owned or controlled by the business that is expected to yield future economic benefits. Example: A restaurant owning its kitchen equipment and delivery van.
2. **Current Assets**: Short-term assets expected to be converted into cash, sold, or consumed within one year. Example: The cash in the register and the inventory sitting on the store shelves.
3. **Non-Current Assets**: Long-term investments, property, and equipment that a company plans to hold for more than one year. Example: A factory building or a 10-year investment in another company's stock.
4. **Cash**: Physical currency and bank balances that are immediately available for use. Example: $500 in the petty cash box and $25,000 in the business checking account.
5. **Inventory**: Raw materials, work-in-progress goods, and finished products held for sale. Example: A clothing store having 200 shirts and 100 pairs of jeans ready for customers to buy.
6. **Accounts Receivable**: Money owed to the company by customers who purchased goods or services on credit. Example: A $1,200 bill sent to a client for consulting work that hasn't been paid yet.
7. **Fixed Assets**: Long-term physical assets used in operations that are not intended for sale. Example: Industrial machinery used to manufacture car parts, which the company plans to keep for 10 years.
8. **Liabilities**: Financial obligations or debts that the company must pay to external parties. Example: A $10,000 bank loan and $2,000 owed to vendors for raw materials.
9. **Current Liabilities**: Short-term obligations that must be paid within one year. Example: Monthly utility bills and the current portion of a long-term loan due next month.
10. **Long-Term Liabilities**: Debts that are due after one year, such as mortgages or multi-year bank loans. Example: A 15-year mortgage on the company's main office building.
11. **Accounts Payable**: Money owed by the company to its suppliers for goods purchased on credit. Example: Owing $500 to a paper supplier for a shipment received last week.
12. **Bank Loans**: Funds borrowed from financial institutions that must be repaid with interest. Example: Taking a $5,000 loan to buy a new server for the IT department.
13. **Equity**: The owner's residual claim on the company's assets after all liabilities are deducted. Example: If assets are $50,000 and liabilities are $30,000, the equity is $20,000.
14. **Owner Capital**: The total amount of money and assets initially invested into the business by its owners. Example: The founder contributing $10,000 of their personal savings to start the shop.
15. **Retained Earnings**: Cumulative profits that have been kept in the business instead of being distributed to owners. Example: Reinvesting $5,000 of this year's profit to buy more inventory for the warehouse.
16. **Financial Position**: The overall financial strength and stability of a business as shown by its balance sheet. Example: Having a high ratio of assets to liabilities, indicating the company is very healthy.
17. **Asset Valuation**: The process of determining the current market or book value of assets listed on the balance sheet. Example: Assessing that an old delivery truck is now worth $5,000 after three years of use.
18. **Debt Management**: Tracking and managing liabilities to ensure the company can meet its financial obligations. Example: Ensuring that monthly loan payments never exceed 10% of total revenue.
19. **Equity Structure**: The specific mix of owner capital, retained earnings, and stock that makes up total equity. Example: A company with $10,000 in owner investment and $40,000 in retained profits.
20. **Balance Sheet Equation**: The fundamental principle stating that Total Assets must always equal Total Liabilities plus Total Equity. Example: If assets increase by $100, either liabilities or equity must also increase by $100.
21. **Cash Equivalents**: Ultra-short-term investments that are easily convertible into cash. Example: A 3-month government bond that is worth exactly its cash value.
22. **Prepaid Expenses**: Payments made in advance for services or goods to be received in the future. Example: Paying for a full year of insurance upfront in January.
23. **Short-Term Investments**: Stocks or bonds the company intends to sell within the next 12 months. Example: Buying $1,000 worth of shares in another company to hold for 6 months.
24. **Marketable Securities**: Financial assets that can be quickly sold on a public exchange for cash. Example: Holding shares of a large public corporation like Apple or Microsoft.
25. **Property Assets**: Land or buildings owned by the company for its own business use. Example: The physical land where the company warehouse is built.
26. **Plant Assets**: Large industrial facilities and permanent structures used in manufacturing. Example: A massive factory building where cars are assembled.
27. **Machinery**: Mechanical equipment and tools used in the production process. Example: A robotic arm used to weld parts together on a production line.
28. **Office Equipment**: Tools used for administrative work, such as computers, printers, and copiers. Example: A $2,000 high-speed office photocopier used by all employees.
29. **Delivery Vehicles**: Trucks, vans, or cars owned by the company to transport goods to customers. Example: A fleet of ten vans used by a bakery to deliver bread to local stores.
30. **Land Ownership**: Title to physical land, which is a non-depreciable asset. Example: Owning a 5-acre plot of land intended for a future factory expansion.
31. **Intangible Assets**: Non-physical assets that provide long-term value, such as patents and trademarks. Example: A secret formula for a soft drink or a well-known brand logo.
32. **Goodwill**: The premium paid for a company above its fair market value during an acquisition. Example: Buying a bakey for $200,000 even though its equipment and building are only worth $150,000.
33. **Patents**: Exclusive rights granted to an inventor to manufacture and sell a new invention. Example: Owning the exclusive right to a new type of battery technology for 20 years.
34. **Trademarks**: Legal protections for brand names, logos, or slogans that identify a company's products. Example: The "Nike Swoosh" or the "Apple" logo being legally protected.
35. **Copyright Assets**: Legal rights protecting original artistic or literary works. Example: A publishing company owning the rights to a best-selling novel series.
36. **Software Systems**: Ownership of digital tools and systems used to run the business. Example: A company owning the custom-built software used to manage its global inventory.
37. **Accumulated Depreciation**: The total amount of value an asset has lost over its life due to wear and tear. Example: A $10,000 machine that has lost $2,000 in value every year for three years.
38. **Net Book Value**: The value of an asset recorded on the balance sheet after subtracting accumulated depreciation. Example: A $10,000 van with $3,000 depreciation has a net book value of $7,000.
39. **Construction in Progress**: Costs incurred for building an asset that is not yet ready for use. Example: Spending $100,000 on the foundation of a new office that is only half-built.
40. **Investment Property**: Real estate owned for the purpose of earning rental income or for its value to increase. Example: Owning an apartment building and collecting rent from the tenants.
41. **Deferred Tax Assets**: Future tax benefits that arise from temporary differences in accounting and tax rules. Example: Paying more tax now than the books show, creating a "credit" for the future.
42. **Long-Term Investments**: Financial assets held specifically for long-term strategic growth. Example: Owning 25% of a supplier company to ensure a steady supply of materials.
43. **Working Capital**: The capital available for day-to-day operations (Current Assets - Current Liabilities). Example: Having $5,000 left over after ensuring all immediate bills can be paid.
44. **Liquidity Position**: The ability of a company to meet its short-term financial obligations with current assets. Example: A company with $10,000 in cash but only $2,000 in bills has a strong liquidity position.
45. **Solvency Position**: The ability of a company to meet its long-term financial commitments and stay in business. Example: Having enough total assets to eventually pay off a 30-year mortgage.
46. **Debt Ratio**: The percentage of a company's total assets that are financed by debt. Example: A 0.4 ratio meaning 40% of everything the company owns was bought with borrowed money.
47. **Debt-to-Equity Ratio**: A measure comparing a company's total liabilities to its total shareholder equity. Example: A ratio of 1.0 meaning the company has equal amounts of debt and owner investment.
48. **Asset Management**: How efficiently a company uses its assets to generate revenue. Example: Making $100,000 in sales using only $50,000 worth of equipment.
49. **Capital Structure**: The specific mix of debt and equity used to finance a company's operations. Example: A company that is 50% funded by bank loans and 50% funded by owner savings.
50. **Financial Stability**: The overall strength of a company's financial health as shown by its balanced sheet. Example: A company that consistently has low debt and high cash reserves.
51. **Treasury Shares**: Shares that were once issued to the public but have been bought back by the company. Example: A company spending $50,000 to buy back its own stock to increase the value of remaining shares.
52. **Additional Paid-In Capital**: The amount shareholders paid for stock above its original "par" or face value. Example: Selling a $1 share for $10, resulting in $9 of additional paid-in capital.
53. **Shareholder Equity**: The total value belonging to the company's owners after all debts are paid. Example: If you sold everything the company owns and paid every bill, this is what is left for the owners.
54. **Common Stock**: Ownership shares that usually give the holder voting rights in company meetings. Example: Owning 100 shares of a company which allows you to vote for the board of directors.
55. **Preferred Stock**: Shares that give holders priority for dividend payments but usually no voting rights. Example: Receiving a fixed $5 dividend every year before common stockholders get anything.
56. **Dividend Distribution**: Paying out a portion of the company's profit to its owners or shareholders. Example: Sending a $50 check to every person who owns a share of the business.
57. **Capital Reserves**: Profit set aside for a specific future purpose, such as buying a new building. Example: Saving $10,000 a month specifically for a factory upgrade in 5 years.
58. **Owner Withdrawals**: When an owner of a non-corporate business takes cash out for personal use. Example: A freelancer taking $2,000 from their business account to pay their personal rent.
59. **Equity Financing**: Raising money for the business by selling ownership shares to new investors. Example: An entrepreneur selling 20% of their company to a venture capitalist for $100,000.
60. **Debt Financing**: Raising money for the business by borrowing from banks or issuing bonds. Example: Taking a $50,000 bank loan that must be paid back with 5% interest.
61. **Asset Turnover Ratio**: A measure of how many dollars in sales are generated by each dollar of assets. Example: A ratio of 2.0 meaning for every $1 of equipment, the company makes $2 in sales.
62. **Return on Assets (ROA)**: A percentage showing how much profit is generated from every dollar of assets. Example: An ROA of 10% meaning the company makes 10 cents of profit for every $1 it owns.
63. **Return on Equity (ROE)**: A measure of how much profit is generated from the owners' investment. Example: An ROE of 20% meaning for every $1 the owners put in, they get 20 cents in profit.
64. **Asset Allocation**: The strategy of dividing a company's investment among different types of assets. Example: Keeping 50% in cash, 30% in inventory, and 20% in long-term bonds.
65. **Financial Risk**: The danger that a company will not be able to meet its debt obligations. Example: Having so much debt that a small drop in sales could lead to bankruptcy.
66. **Balance Sheet Analysis**: Using ratios and trends to evaluate a company's financial health over time. Example: Comparing this year's balance sheet to last year's to see if debt is decreasing.
67. **Financial Reporting**: The process of providing financial statements to owners, investors, and regulators. Example: Publishing a yearly report that includes the balance sheet and profit and loss statement.
68. **Corporate Transparency**: Providing clear and honest financial information to all stakeholders. Example: Clearly listing all company debts in the annual report so investors know the risks.
69. **Investor Evaluation**: When potential buyers analyze a balance sheet before deciding to invest. Example: A bank reviewing a business's assets before deciding whether to give them a loan.
70. **Credit Evaluation**: When suppliers check a company's balance sheet before allowing them to buy on credit. Example: A vendor refusing to ship goods until they see that the company has enough cash to pay.
71. **Asset Growth**: The increase in the total value of what a company owns over a period of time. Example: A company's assets growing from $100,000 to $150,000 in one year due to new equipment.
72. **Debt Reduction**: The process of paying off liabilities to improve the company's financial position. Example: Paying off a $10,000 loan early to save on interest costs.
73. **Equity Growth**: The increase in owner value through reinvesting profit or getting more investment. Example: Retained earnings growing from $5,000 to $10,000 because profit wasn't taken out of the business.
74. **Asset Liquidity Ranking**: Listing assets in order of how quickly they can be turned into cash. Example: Listing Cash first, then Accounts Receivable, then Inventory, and finally Buildings.
75. **Financial Health Indicator**: Using balance sheet data to predict whether a company will succeed or fail. Example: A high current ratio which suggests the company is very unlikely to go broke soon.
76. **Capital Investment**: Spending money specifically to buy long-term assets that will grow the business. Example: Spending $20,000 on a new high-speed manufacturing machine.
77. **Asset Utilization**: Measuring how effectively a company is using its specific tools and equipment. Example: Ensuring a delivery truck is making deliveries 8 hours a day rather than sitting idle.
78. **Balance Sheet Reporting Period**: The specific date and time when the financial snapshot was taken. Example: A balance sheet titled "As of December 31, 2023".
79. **Historical Cost Principle**: The rule that assets must be recorded at their original purchase price. Example: Keeping a building at $100,000 on the books even if its market price is now $500,000.
80. **Fair Value Measurement**: Estimating the current market price of an asset, often for investments. Example: Updating the value of shares in another company based on today's stock market price.
81. **Financial Leverage**: Using borrowed money (debt) to try and increase the potential return for owners. Example: Borrowing $100,00 at 5% interest to fund a project that makes 15% profit.
82. **Asset Impairment**: A permanent reduction in the value of an asset because it is damaged or outdated. Example: Writing off $2,000 from the value of a laptop because the screen is cracked.
83. **Equity Dilution**: When a company issues new shares, decreasing the percentage owned by current owners. Example: An owner who had 100% now has 80% because they sold some shares to a new partner.
84. **Asset Revaluation**: Formally adjusting the book value of an asset to match its current market value. Example: Hiring a professional appraiser to update the value of the company's land on the books.
85. **Liability Settlement**: The final payment of an outstanding debt, removing it from the balance sheet. Example: Making the final $1,000 payment on a truck loan so it is now fully owned.
86. **Equity Contribution**: When an owner puts more of their personal money into the business bank account. Example: An owner adding $5,000 of their own money to cover a temporary cash shortage.
87. **Financial Strategy**: Using balance sheet ratios to make long-term decisions about growth and risk. Example: Deciding to stop borrowing money and focus on growing through reinvesting profit.
88. **Asset Replacement**: Selling an old asset and using the money (plus more) to buy a newer one. Example: Trading in a 10-year-old delivery van for a brand new electric model.
89. **Capital Expansion**: Increasing the company's total resources and assets to support larger operations. Example: Buying a second warehouse to double the amount of inventory the company can hold.
90. **Balance Sheet Monitoring**: Regularly checking the relationship between assets and debt to stay proactive. Example: Reviewing the balance sheet every Friday to ensure there is always enough cash for bills.
91. **Accounting Period End**: The final day of a reporting cycle when all accounts are balanced. Example: Closing the books on June 30th to prepare the mid-year financial reports.
92. **Audited Balance Sheet**: A balance sheet that has been officially verified by an outside accounting firm. Example: A public company hiring a CPA to prove their assets and debts are listed correctly.
93. **Internal Financial Review**: When company management looks at the balance sheet to judge performance. Example: The CEO and CFO meeting every month to discuss why liabilities have increased.
94. **Asset Protection**: Safeguarding what the company owns from theft, damage, or legal claims. Example: Buying a high-quality safe for physical cash and insurance for the warehouse.
95. **Debt Monitoring**: Tracking the exact amount owed to every single bank and supplier. Example: Keeping a detailed list of all 20 vendors and exactly when each bill is due.
96. **Shareholder Value**: The total wealth created for investors through profit and company growth. Example: A company's equity growing from $1 million to $2 million over five years.
97. **Financial Sustainability**: The ability of a business to maintain its operations without needing more loans. Example: A business that makes enough profit to pay for its own new equipment and expansion.
98. **Regulatory Compliance**: Ensuring that the balance sheet follows all local and international laws. Example: Following the GAAP or IFRS rules when categorizing assets and liabilities.
99. **Stakeholder Confidence**: The level of trust that banks, owners, and employees have in the company. Example: A banks being very willing to lend money because the company has a "strong" balance sheet.
100. **Balanced Financial Structure**: The final state where assets equal liabilities plus equity, indicating a complete snapshot. Example: Having $100,000 in assets which is perfectly explained by $40,000 in debt and $60,000 in owner value.
